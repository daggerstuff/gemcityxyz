There's a dangerous myth in tech that our work is somehow neutral. That code is just code, algorithms are just math, and the political implications are someone else's problem.

This is bullshit.

## Every System Has Values Baked In

When you build a recommendation algorithm that optimizes for engagement, you're making a political choice: engagement over wellbeing. When you design a surveillance system for "efficiency," you're choosing efficiency over privacy. When you automate hiring with a model trained on historical data, you're codifying historical bias.

<Callout variant="tip">
The "we're just building tools" defense crumbles when you realize tools shape behavior. A hammer doesn't just drive nails — it creates a world where problems look like nails.
</Callout>

## The Surveillance Economy

Take a look at [acab.lol](https://acab.lol) — my ongoing exploration of how police technology and corporate surveillance have converged. It's the same infrastructure, the same vendors, the same logic of control rebranded for different markets.

The tech that tracks "engagement metrics" for advertisers is the same tech that tracks "patterns of interest" for law enforcement. The facial recognition that unlocks your phone is the facial recognition that identifies protesters.

You can't build for one without enabling the other.

## Toward Liberation Technology

So what does tech for the people look like?

**Decentralized** — Not because blockchain is cool, but because centralized power is dangerous. Systems that work when servers get seized, when domains get blocked, when corporations pivot.

**Transparent** — Open source isn't just about code quality. It's about accountability. You can't hide surveillance in open source.

**Human-scale** — Technology that augments human capability rather than replacing human judgment. Tools for organizers, not automation for executives.

**Sustainable** — Built to last, not to scale. Community-owned, not VC-funded. Surviving the long haul rather than burning out in pursuit of hockey-stick growth.

## The Hard Part

Here's the uncomfortable truth: liberation tech is often harder to build. It requires:

- Thinking about edge cases like "what if the user is undocumented"
- Designing for users with intermittent connectivity
- Building for trust rather than engagement
- Saying no to profitable surveillance

But that's the work. That's the craft.
